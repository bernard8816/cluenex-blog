RSI (Relative Strength Index) is a momentum oscillator that measures the speed and magnitude of price changes on a scale of 0 to 100, used to identify overbought and oversold conditions in a stock. Developed by J. Welles Wilder Jr. in 1978, RSI compares the magnitude of recent gains to recent losses over a defined lookback period — most commonly 14 days. It is one of the most widely used technical indicators in equities, forex, and crypto trading.

## How RSI Works

RSI is calculated using the formula:

```
RSI = 100 − (100 / (1 + RS))
RS = Average Gain over N periods / Average Loss over N periods
```

The default lookback period is 14 candles. Shorter periods (e.g. 7) make RSI more sensitive and produce more signals. Longer periods (e.g. 21) smooth out noise and produce fewer but more reliable signals.

RSI readings above 70 are conventionally interpreted as overbought, while readings below 30 indicate oversold conditions. The 50 level acts as a midline — RSI above 50 suggests bullish momentum, below 50 suggests bearish momentum.

## How to Use RSI in Practice

1. **Identify overbought and oversold extremes**. Look for RSI above 70 (potential pullback) or below 30 (potential bounce). These are starting points, not standalone signals.

2. **Watch for RSI divergence**. If price makes a higher high but RSI makes a lower high, that's bearish divergence — momentum is fading even as price rises. The reverse (price lower low, RSI higher low) is bullish divergence.

3. **Use the 50 line as a trend filter**. In uptrends, RSI tends to stay above 40–50 and bounce off it. In downtrends, RSI tends to stay below 50–60 and reject from it.

4. **Confirm with volume and price action**. RSI is a momentum indicator, not a standalone trading system. A bullish divergence combined with rising volume and a support bounce is far stronger than divergence alone.

5. **Adjust thresholds for trending markets**. In strong uptrends, use 80 as overbought and 40 as oversold. In strong downtrends, use 60 as overbought and 20 as oversold.

## Common Mistakes and Misconceptions

**"Overbought means sell, oversold means buy."** Many traders treat RSI extremes as automatic reversal signals. In reality, strong trends can keep RSI pinned above 70 or below 30 for weeks. Selling every overbought reading in a bull market is a losing strategy.

**"RSI works the same on all timeframes."** RSI signals on a 1-minute chart are noise compared to RSI signals on a daily chart. Higher timeframe RSI carries more weight. A daily RSI divergence is a far more significant signal than a 5-minute RSI divergence.

**"All RSI divergences lead to reversals."** Only about 40–50% of divergences result in meaningful reversals. Divergence is a warning, not a guarantee. Always wait for price confirmation — a break of structure, a candlestick reversal pattern, or a moving average cross — before acting.

**"RSI is enough on its own."** RSI is a single dimension of analysis. Combining it with volume, market structure, and sentiment dramatically improves signal quality.

## Example: Using RSI on NVDA

Consider a hypothetical scenario where NVDA has rallied from $400 to $520 over three weeks, with daily RSI now reading 78. Looking at the chart:

- RSI is above 70 (overbought)
- But the most recent two daily highs in price are accompanied by lower RSI highs (bearish divergence)
- Volume on the most recent push to $520 was 30% lower than the prior breakout

This is a high-conviction setup for a pullback. A trader might tighten stops, take partial profits, or look for bearish entries on a break below short-term support. The combination of overbought RSI + bearish divergence + declining volume is the kind of multi-signal confluence that makes RSI useful.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What does it mean when RSI is above 70?**
When RSI rises above 70, the stock is considered overbought, meaning price has risen faster than typical and may be due for a pullback or consolidation. However, in strong uptrends, RSI can remain above 70 for weeks without a reversal — traders should confirm with additional signals before taking action.

**Is RSI a leading or lagging indicator?**
RSI is technically a lagging indicator because it's calculated from past price data. However, RSI divergence can act as a leading signal because momentum often fades before price reverses, giving observant traders an early warning.

**What is the best RSI setting for day trading?**
Day traders often use shorter RSI periods like 7 or 9 to capture faster momentum shifts. Swing traders typically stick with the default 14-period setting. The trade-off is sensitivity vs. reliability — shorter periods give more signals but more false ones.

**How is RSI different from Stochastic RSI?**
RSI measures price momentum directly. Stochastic RSI applies the Stochastic formula to RSI values, making it more sensitive and faster-reacting. Stochastic RSI oscillates between 0 and 100 more rapidly, producing more frequent overbought/oversold signals.

**Can RSI be used for crypto?**
Yes. RSI works on any liquid asset class with continuous price data, including crypto, forex, futures, and commodities. The same 30/70 thresholds and divergence principles apply, though crypto's higher volatility often warrants wider thresholds (e.g. 20/80).

**Why does RSI stay above 70 for so long sometimes?**
In strong trending markets, momentum stays elevated because buyers keep stepping in on every dip. This is why blindly shorting overbought RSI fails in bull markets. Use RSI as a warning to tighten risk management, not as a standalone reversal signal.
